Motivation

Why Does Cooperation Exist?

  • In evolution, selfish behaviour typically outcompetes altruistic behaviour
  • Yet cooperation is ubiquitous — bacteria, social insects, animal groups, human societies
  • Classical game theory: rational agents should always defect (the "Prisoner's Dilemma")
  • In large, well-mixed populations, defection is the only evolutionarily stable strategy
  • The puzzle: how can cooperation persist against rational selfishness?
Background

The Prisoner's Dilemma

Cooperate (C) Defect (D)
Cooperate (C) R = 1  |  R = 1 S = 0  |  T = b
Defect (D) T = b  |  S = 0 P = 0  |  P = 0

Nowak & May reduce the PD to a single parameter b > 1: the temptation to defect. Defection is always the dominant strategy — yet mutual cooperation is better for both.

The Problem

Classical Theory:
Defection Always Wins

  • Any defector in a cooperating population earns a higher payoff
  • Natural selection favours defectors — they spread and take over
  • Defection is the only ESS in infinite, well-mixed populations
  • (Axelrod & Hamilton 1981) — cooperation can survive via tit-for-tat but requires memory & repeated interaction
  • Nowak & May ask — can cooperation survive with none of these? No memory. No repetition. Just space
  • Classical prediction: cooperation should collapse entirely
Invasion of a cooperator population
Key Innovation

What If Space Matters?

Classical: Well-Mixed
Nowak & May: Spatial Grid

Each individual interacts only with its 8 immediate neighbours. This single structural change transforms the evolutionary outcome entirely.

Methodology

The Cellular Automaton Model

  • Grid: N × N lattice, fixed boundaries (results hold for toroidal too)
  • States: each cell is a Cooperator (C) or Defector (D)
  • Interactions: each cell plays the PD with its 8 neighbours and itself
  • Score: sum of payoffs across all 9 interactions
  • Update rule: adopt the strategy of the highest-scoring neighbour — synchronous
  • Initial condition: single D in a sea of C (or random)
Payoff summary (row player)

C vs C → +1 each
C vs D → C: 0, D: +b
D vs D → 0 each

Mechanism

Why Does Space Enable Cooperation?

  • Cooperators form clusters — interacting mainly with each other, earning high mutual payoffs
  • Defectors lurk at boundaries — they exploit edge cooperators, but are also surrounded by other defectors (zero payoff)
  • The interior is unreachable — cooperators deep inside clusters are protected; the cluster acts as a fortress
  • "Spatial chaos" = perpetual fluctuation at boundaries — neither side ever takes over
Cluster dynamics
"Cooperators and defectors can coexist in a spatially structured world, even when defectors would completely eliminate cooperators in an unstructured world."

Conclusion

The World Is Not Well-Mixed

  • A simple cellular automaton — one free parameter b — produces rich, unexpected dynamics
  • Spatial structure alone sustains cooperation against defection — no memory, kinship, or reputation needed
  • Directly overturns a central prediction of classical evolutionary game theory
  • Symmetric starting conditions produce dynamic fractals — an "evolutionary kaleidoscope" (Fig. 3)
  • Simulation was essential — the chaotic spatial patterns are not analytically tractable
The patterns "have an aesthetic and mathematical interest of their own" — and may reflect dynamics of spatially extended systems across biology, physics and chemistry. (Nowak & May, 1992)
